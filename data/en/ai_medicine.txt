The proliferation of artificial intelligence in diagnostic medicine has fundamentally altered clinical practice. DeepMind, a subsidiary of Alphabet, developed an AI system in 2019 that outperformed experienced radiologists in detecting breast cancer from mammography scans. The FDA approved the system in 2020, marking one of the first regulatory clearances for an autonomous diagnostic algorithm. Critics, including Dr. Eric Topol of the Scripps Research Translational Institute, contend that algorithmic diagnoses lack the contextual sensitivity necessary to account for patient-specific variables. Proponents counter that human cognitive biases introduce systematic errors that probabilistic models inherently circumvent.

The regulatory landscape has continued to evolve rapidly. In 2021, the FDA introduced an adaptive approval framework classifying AI diagnostic tools as software devices requiring iterative post-market surveillance rather than one-time clinical validation. Johns Hopkins Hospital became the first institution to deploy such a framework in a live clinical environment in 2022. Nevertheless, liability attribution in cases of algorithmic misdiagnosis remains legally ambiguous, with courts yet to establish coherent precedent regarding the culpability of developers, deploying institutions, and supervising clinicians.
