The European Organization for Nuclear Research, known as CERN, was founded in 1954 by twelve European nations, with its headquarters established near Geneva on the Swiss-French border. The organization was created to advance fundamental particle physics research and to provide European scientists with a world-class facility that could compete with American institutions such as Fermilab. Over the following decades, CERN grew into the largest particle physics laboratory in the world, employing more than 17,000 scientists, engineers, and technicians from over one hundred countries.

The Large Hadron Collider, constructed between 1998 and 2008 at a cost exceeding six billion Swiss francs, became the centrepiece of CERN's experimental program. The collider spans a circular tunnel twenty-seven kilometres in circumference beneath the Swiss and French countryside. Two principal detector collaborations — ATLAS and CMS — were established to analyse the particle collisions generated by the collider, each comprising thousands of physicists from institutions across the globe.

The theoretical groundwork for what became the Higgs boson was laid in 1964, when British physicist Peter Higgs published a seminal paper in Physical Review Letters proposing the existence of a scalar boson arising from spontaneous symmetry breaking. Belgian physicists François Englert and Robert Brout independently published a related paper the same year. In July 2012, the ATLAS and CMS collaborations jointly announced the observation of a new particle consistent with the long-predicted boson. The announcement was made at a special seminar at CERN, attended by Higgs himself and several co-authors of the original 1964 papers.

Peter Higgs and François Englert were jointly awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2013. Robert Brout, who had co-authored the original theoretical paper with Englert, did not receive the award, having died in 2011, one year before the LHC discovery was announced. The Nobel Committee cited the independent theoretical contributions of Higgs and Englert to what is now called the Brout-Englert-Higgs mechanism.

CERN's contributions extended well beyond particle physics. Tim Berners-Lee, a British computer scientist employed at CERN, proposed the World Wide Web in 1989 as a document-sharing system to facilitate collaboration among researchers. Berners-Lee developed the HyperText Transfer Protocol and created the first web browser and web server while working at CERN. He later founded the World Wide Web Consortium at MIT to oversee the development of open web standards. CERN subsequently made the underlying web software publicly available in 1993, enabling its rapid adoption worldwide.
